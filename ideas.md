# Dice
So if you just increase hit modifiers, eventually people always hit. Then the only thing that matters is damage.

You can roll a d100 and it has a flat chance of getting any roll. A larger dice would mean possibilities exist while each individual modifier matters less (ex: there's no breakpoints where things start accelerating)

The more dice that are rolled the more normalized the curve becomes (reduced standard deviation).

## Keeping an expected hit rate
If you just increase AC by the player's expected modifier given a level, you can keep the hit rates similar. This reduces the ability of sub-standard builds to play while also decreasing the "satisfaction" of building an optimized character.

## Alternative scaling methods
A toughness die could help create a feeling of tankiness when it becomes difficult to further increase something like AC. It can also create a different feeling between someone who is hard to hit versus someone who is tough.

Additional damage dice create the ability to scale damage even when hits are few, or when hits are guaranteed.

Crits ranges can keep dice rolls relevant even when hits are/aren't guaranteed.

Systems like fatigue or wounds can create a rhythm to the battle where the nature of the fight changes as time goes on (ex: as people get more tired, they hit less or defend less). Wounds would be like even glancing blows (in systems of degrees of success) can translate into something meaningful.

### Note: Targeting
Consider what the role of the dice would be.
- Where on the bell curve does it affect? High? Low?
- Does it skew the curve towards one end?
- Does it further emphasize one end? (Ex: roll twice and average skew the center of the curve)
- What's the target effectiveness? (ex: an increase of 5%? 15%? 50%?)
